Venezuela's story might be the most jaw-dropping of the three. This is a country sitting on the largest proven oil reserves on the planet — over 303 billion barrels, more than Saudi Arabia. In the 1950s, it had the fourth-highest GDP per capita in the world. Twice as rich as Chile. Four times richer than Japan. Twelve times richer than China. It was called "the millionaire of the Americas."

Today, its GDP has shrunk by roughly 75 to 80 percent. Nearly one in four Venezuelans has fled the country. Hyperinflation peaked at an estimated 300,000 percent. A country with more oil than anyone else on Earth — and it fell apart.

How? That's what we're here to unpack.

Let's start with the country itself.

Venezuela sits on the northern tip of South America, facing the Caribbean Sea. It's about 354,000 square miles — roughly twice the size of California. The terrain is remarkably diverse. The Andes Mountains extend into the west, with peaks reaching nearly 16,000 feet. The center is dominated by the Llanos — vast tropical grasslands, South America's version of the Great Plains. The south is Amazon rainforest, with those extraordinary flat-topped mountains called tepuis — the word means "house of the gods" in the indigenous Pemon language.

One of those tepuis is home to Angel Falls — the world's tallest uninterrupted waterfall at 3,212 feet, roughly 20 times the height of Niagara Falls. If you've seen the Pixar movie "Up," the fictional "Paradise Falls" was modeled on Angel Falls.

Neighbors: Colombia to the west, Brazil to the south, Guyana to the east. There's actually an unresolved territorial dispute with Guyana — Venezuela claims over two-thirds of Guyana's territory, and held a referendum on annexation in late 2023, though the International Court of Justice ordered Venezuela not to change the status quo.

Population was about 30 million before the crisis. Now it's closer to 28 to 29 million, due to the massive exodus we'll discuss later. About 51 percent are mestizo — mixed European and indigenous — and 43 percent identify as white, mainly of Spanish, Italian, and Portuguese descent. That Italian and Portuguese element is important — it reflects a period when Europeans were immigrating to Venezuela, not the other way around. Yes, Venezuela was once an immigration destination.

The name "Venezuela" traditionally means "Little Venice" — Spanish explorers in 1499 saw indigenous stilt houses on Lake Maracaibo and were reminded of Venice. That lake, by the way, is the heart of Venezuela's oil industry.

Oh, and one more thing before we get to the heavy stuff. Beauty pageants.

Venezuela is the most successful beauty pageant country in the world. Twenty-four titles across the Big Four international competitions — seven Miss Universe, six Miss World, nine Miss International, two Miss Earth. More than any other nation. The annual Miss Venezuela competition is the most-watched TV program in the country. Behind this is a man named Osmel Sousa — nicknamed "the Beauty Czar" — who since 1981 has run what amounts to an industrial-scale beauty manufacturing system. Scouts, coaches, stylists, surgeons — every "imperfection" cataloged and systematically addressed. In a country where the economy collapsed, beauty pageant victories became one of the few sources of collective national pride.

Now let's talk about the golden era.

According to the World Economic Forum, by 1950, Venezuela's per capita GDP ranked fourth globally. Oil extraction had begun in the 1920s at Lake Maracaibo, and by the 1940s Venezuela was the world's second-largest oil producer after the United States. The money was enormous. Europeans fleeing post-war poverty — Italians, Spaniards, Portuguese — emigrated to Venezuela by the hundreds of thousands, just as people today emigrate to the US and Canada.

Politically, Venezuela was also ahead of the curve. After overthrowing military dictator Marcos Perez Jimenez in 1958, three major parties signed the Punto Fijo Pact — a power-sharing agreement that produced the most stable democratic period in Venezuelan history. While the rest of Latin America lurched from coup to coup, Venezuela peacefully alternated power through elections for decades.

So from 1958 to the early 1980s, Venezuela was almost a model country — oil wealth, democratic stability, vibrant immigration. If you'd told a Venezuelan in 1980 that their country would experience the worst non-war economic collapse in modern history, they'd have thought you were insane.

What went wrong? Two words: oil curse.

The "resource curse" is an economics term for a paradox: countries with abundant natural resources often develop worse than those without. Why? Because when you can get rich just by pumping stuff out of the ground, you have no incentive to develop other industries. Your entire economy becomes a one-legged stool. Oil prices high? Everything's great. Oil prices drop? Everything collapses.

And massive oil wealth creates massive corruption. When the government controls the main revenue source, whoever controls the government controls the money. Political competition becomes a fight over the oil pie, not over who can govern better. The Punto Fijo two-party democracy gradually degenerated into a patronage system.

The turning point came on February 18, 1983 — "Black Friday," or Viernes Negro. The government was forced to devalue the bolivar from 4.3 to 7.5 per dollar. Purchasing power dropped about 75 percent almost overnight. Every Venezuelan remembers that date. It marks the end of the golden age.

Then came February 27, 1989 — the Caracazo. President Carlos Andres Perez implemented IMF austerity measures. Gas prices and transit fares shot up. Riots and looting erupted across Caracas. The military was deployed. Death toll estimates range from hundreds to over 3,000. The Caracazo shattered public trust in the democratic establishment.

A banking crisis in 1994 destroyed what was left of public confidence.

By the late 1990s, Venezuela was a powder keg — still oil-rich on paper, but with massive inequality, rampant corruption, a shrinking middle class, and millions who could see the wealth but never touched it.

Enter Hugo Chavez.

Born in 1954 to a modest family in western Venezuela, Chavez was a military officer who idolized Simon Bolivar, the liberator of South America. In 1992, he led a military coup against President Perez. It failed. He was captured. But in a famous televised moment, he told his followers: the objectives had not been achieved "por ahora" — for now.

"For now." Those two words turned a failed coup leader into a folk hero. People heard not surrender but a promise: this isn't over. In a country sick of corrupt politicians, this soldier who dared to challenge the system — and owned his failure without giving up — became a symbol of hope.

Released after two years, Chavez won the 1998 presidential election with 56 percent of the vote, ending the Punto Fijo era.

Chavez's "Bolivarian Revolution" had a simple core idea: use oil wealth to eliminate poverty. He launched social programs called "missions" — free healthcare clinics staffed by Cuban doctors, literacy campaigns, subsidized food stores, housing construction. During the oil boom years, especially 2004 to 2008, poverty dropped by about 20 percentage points. Among the poor, Chavez achieved near-religious devotion.

But simultaneously, he was doing things that would prove catastrophic.

First, he gutted PDVSA — the national oil company. After a 2002-2003 strike by PDVSA management, Chavez fired approximately 18,000 employees — including the vast majority of experienced engineers, geologists, and managers — replacing them with political loyalists who lacked technical expertise. A world-class oil company lost decades of institutional knowledge overnight. This single decision may be the most destructive act in Venezuela's modern history.

Second, mass nationalization. Beyond oil, Chavez nationalized telecommunications, electricity, steel, cement, and food processing. Every nationalization drove away private investment. Every nationalized company became less efficient under government management.

Third, he systematically dismantled independent institutions — courts packed with loyalists, central bank stripped of independence, opposition politicians prosecuted.

His foreign policy was equally dramatic. Cuba received about 100,000 barrels per day of subsidized oil in exchange for doctors, teachers, and — crucially — intelligence and security advisors. Russia provided 2.9 billion dollars in arms and at least 17 billion in total loans. And China — according to AidData at William & Mary, one of the most authoritative trackers of Chinese overseas lending — committed approximately 105.6 billion dollars in loans to Venezuela between 2000 and 2023, about 95 billion of it backed by future oil deliveries. China stopped new lending in 2016 as the economy collapsed.

There's even a flight that captures the absurdity of these alliances. Venezuela's state airline Conviasa operates an Airbus A340 route: Caracas to Havana to Moscow to Guangzhou. Once or twice a week. Commercially viable? Not even close. It's a political statement in the sky — connecting four anti-Western allied states, carefully avoiding airspace that might restrict Venezuelan aircraft.

Chavez died of cancer on March 5, 2013, at age 58. He was treated in Cuba — not in Venezuela's own hospitals. He named Nicolas Maduro as his successor. Maduro won a snap election by a razor-thin 50.6 percent.

If Chavez was the slow walk to the cliff's edge, Maduro was the jump.

What followed has been described by the Economics Observatory as "the single largest economic collapse outside of war in at least 45 years."

GDP shrank roughly 75 to 80 percent between 2013 and 2021 — from 373 billion dollars to about 43 billion. Hyperinflation: 800 percent in 2016, 4,000 percent in 2017, 130,000 percent in 2018, an estimated 300,000 percent in 2019. Oil production collapsed from 3.3 million barrels per day in the late 1990s to under a million by 2025 — on top of the world's largest reserves.

The humanitarian numbers are devastating. The minimum wage is about 4 dollars per month. The basic family food basket costs about 540 dollars. Eighty-nine percent of households report food insecurity. Seventy percent of hospitals lack regular water and electricity. Fifty-six percent of hospital beds are inoperable. Diseases like malaria surged 900 percent between 2007 and 2017.

In July 2024, a disputed election saw the opposition obtain tallies showing their candidate winning with about 67 percent — but the government declared Maduro the winner. Then on January 3, 2026, the United States military captured Maduro in an overnight operation. He's currently in a New York federal jail awaiting trial on drug trafficking charges.

And then there's the exodus. According to UNHCR, over 7.9 million Venezuelans have left since 2014 — roughly one in four. Colombia alone has absorbed about 3 million. Hundreds of thousands have made the horrifying journey through the Darien Gap — a 66-mile stretch of roadless jungle between Colombia and Panama — facing drowning, wildlife, bandits, and sexual violence. In 2023, a record 520,000 people crossed it, with Venezuelans making up 63 percent.

So what have we learned from Iran, Cuba, and Venezuela — three countries that went from prosperity to despair?

The patterns are striking. All three were relatively wealthy. All three experienced a form of revolution born from genuine anger at inequality and corruption. All three saw a charismatic leader use anti-American sentiment as a core narrative. All three initially improved conditions for the poor. And all three saw power concentration, institutional destruction, economic mismanagement, and corruption lead to collapse — followed by massive brain drain.

Five lessons stand out.

One: institutions matter more than leaders. A good leader brings temporary prosperity, but without independent courts, free media, and transparent elections, good outcomes can't last and bad leaders can't be peacefully replaced.

Two: economic diversification is survival. Depending on a single resource — oil, sugar, Soviet subsidies — is sitting on a one-legged stool.

Three: populist promises are often poison. "I'll destroy the corrupt elite for you" can be sincere, but if it comes with dismantling institutions and concentrating power, the cure is worse than the disease.

Four: once brain drain starts, it creates a vicious cycle that's nearly impossible to reverse.

Five: ordinary people always pay the price. Politicians play power games, great powers move pieces on geopolitical chessboards, and the people who just want a normal life — the mother in the bread line, the father crossing the jungle, the young person starting over in a foreign country — they bear the cost.

According to Foreign Affairs, even in the most optimistic scenario — strong oil recovery, half of emigrants returning — Venezuela's GDP would still be 50 percent below its 2013 peak after a decade. This isn't a quick fix. It's generational.

Is there hope? If you look at the 67 percent who voted for change in 2024, if you look at the millions of Venezuelans abroad building skills and capital, if you look at the stubborn refusal of young Venezuelans to accept the status quo — yes, hope exists. But between hope and reality stretches a very long, very hard road.
