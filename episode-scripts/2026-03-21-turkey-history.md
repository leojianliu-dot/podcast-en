Ninety-seven percent of its territory is in Asia. But it spent a century trying to become European. It's overwhelmingly Muslim — but it was once the most radically secular country in the Islamic world. Its biggest city literally sits on two continents — you can have breakfast in Asia, walk across a bridge, and have coffee in Europe. It's been hit with devastating inflation and currency collapse — but the economy keeps growing, tourists keep coming, and life goes on.

And for our series, Turkey is the mirror image of Iran. Two Muslim-majority countries, similar populations, both had modernizing secular leaders in the twentieth century. One ended up a theocracy. The other didn't — at least not yet. Why? That question is at the heart of today's episode.

But first, some things you probably don't know about Turkey.

Santa Claus is Turkish. Well, sort of. Saint Nicholas — the historical figure behind Santa — was a Greek Christian bishop who lived in Myra, which is modern-day Demre in southern Turkey, in the fourth century. His tradition of secret gift-giving eventually traveled to the Netherlands, then to New York, and became Santa Claus.

Tulips are Turkish, not Dutch. They originated in Central Asia and were extensively cultivated by the Ottoman Turks. Sultan Suleiman the Magnificent was obsessed with them. The Ottomans had over 1,500 varieties and an entire era named after them — the Tulip Period. The Dutch made tulips commercially famous, but they got them from Turkey in the sixteenth century.

Turkey is the world's number one tea-drinking nation. Per capita consumption is 3 to 4.6 kilograms per year — nearly double Ireland in second place. The average Turk drinks 1,300 cups annually, up to ten a day in winter. Always served in distinctive tulip-shaped glass cups.

The oldest known man-made temple is in Turkey. Gobekli Tepe — built around 9,600 BC by hunter-gatherers with no metal tools. That's 6,000 years older than Stonehenge. Massive T-shaped limestone pillars, 18 feet tall. It fundamentally changed our understanding of when humans started building monumental structures.

Istanbul's Grand Bazaar, built in 1455, has over 4,000 shops across 61 covered streets and receives up to 400,000 visitors a day.

And here's one that blows my mind: Hagia Sophia has had four lives. Built in 537 as the world's largest Christian cathedral. Served as an Orthodox church for a thousand years. Converted to a mosque in 1453 when the Ottomans conquered Constantinople. Turned into a museum by Ataturk in 1935. Converted back into a mosque by Erdogan in 2020. During prayers, motorized blinds now cover the Christian mosaics, then retract when prayers end.

Okay. Now let's get into the real story.

Turkey covers about 302,000 square miles — roughly twice the size of California. Population: about 86 million. The capital is Ankara — not Istanbul, though Istanbul is the largest city and economic heart.

The critical geography fact: 97 percent of Turkey is in Asia — the Anatolian plateau. Only 3 percent is in Europe — a region called Thrace. Despite that, about 10 percent of the population lives on the European side. And Istanbul sits right on the dividing line — the Bosphorus Strait, 19 miles long, with three bridges and an undersea rail tunnel connecting Asia to Europe. It is the only major city in the world that straddles two continents.

Now let's rewind — way back — to before Turkey was Turkish.

Anatolia — the Asian landmass that makes up most of modern Turkey — has an ancient history that predates the Turks by thousands of years. The Hittites were here in the Bronze Age. Troy was here — yes, Homer's Trojan War happened in what's now Turkey's northwest corner. Ancient Greeks colonized the western coast — Ephesus, Miletus, Halicarnassus — turning it into one of the most intellectually productive regions of the ancient world.

After Alexander the Great, Anatolia became deeply Hellenized. Then came Rome. In 330 AD, Emperor Constantine built Constantinople on the site of the ancient Greek city of Byzantium — modern Istanbul. For roughly the next thousand years, Constantinople was the center of the Christian world. Hagia Sophia was Christendom's largest cathedral. The people spoke Greek. They worshipped Christ.

This is the land the Turks inherited. A Greek, Christian, Roman land. Understanding that starting point makes what happened next all the more remarkable.

In 1071, the Seljuk Turks — a Turkic people from Central Asia — defeated the Byzantine army at the Battle of Manzikert in eastern Turkey. This opened Anatolia's interior to waves of Turkic migration. Over the next roughly 400 years, through intermarriage, Sufi missionary work, economic incentives, and cultural assimilation, Anatolia transformed from a Greek Christian land into a Turkish Muslim one. By the mid-1400s, about 85 percent of Anatolia had converted to Islam. Not overnight. Not by the sword alone. But completely.

The vehicle of that transformation was the Ottoman Empire — one of the longest-lasting and most powerful empires in history. It started as a tiny chieftaincy in the late 1200s and grew into a colossus. The defining moment came in 1453, when 21-year-old Sultan Mehmed the Conqueror took Constantinople after a 55-day siege using cutting-edge gunpowder cannons. That day ended the Roman Empire — roughly 1,500 years after Augustus.

At its peak around 1683, the Ottoman Empire controlled 2 million square miles — southeastern Europe up to the gates of Vienna, the entire Balkans, Greece, Hungary, most of the Middle East, Egypt, and North Africa. Bulgaria, which we covered last episode, spent 500 years under Ottoman rule. This was the empire that ruled them.

The Ottomans achieved extraordinary things in architecture, art, science, and medicine. But history also records darkness. In 1915 to 1916, the Ottoman government carried out the systematic destruction of its Armenian Christian population — through massacres, death marches into the Syrian desert, and forced conversion. Estimates range from 664,000 to 1.2 million killed. Over 20 countries recognize it as genocide. Turkey still refuses to use that word. This is documented by the US Holocaust Memorial Museum and is considered the first genocide of the twentieth century.

After World War One, the Ottoman Empire collapsed. Its Middle Eastern territories were carved up by Britain and France. And from the wreckage rose a man named Mustafa Kemal — later called Ataturk, "Father of the Turks."

What Ataturk did in the 1920s and 30s may be the most radical top-down transformation any country has ever undergone. In barely a decade, he abolished the Islamic caliphate. Closed religious schools. Replaced the Arabic alphabet with Latin letters — overnight, the entire writing system changed. Adopted the Western calendar. Introduced European-style civil, commercial, and criminal law. Banned the traditional fez hat. Discouraged women's veiling. Gave women the right to vote in 1934 — before France or Italy. Ended Islamic polygamy. Introduced civil marriage. Adopted the metric system.

No other Muslim-majority country has ever attempted anything comparable in speed and scope.

And here's where the Iran comparison becomes essential.

Iran's Shah tried something similar — modernization, Westernization, women's rights. But in 1979, an Islamic revolution overthrew him and installed a theocracy. Why did the same approach survive in Turkey for almost 80 years but fail in Iran?

The biggest difference: the military. Turkey's army positioned itself as the guardian of secularism. When politics drifted toward Islamism, the military intervened — coups in 1960, 1971, 1980, and a "soft coup" in 1997. Each time, the stated purpose was protecting Ataturk's legacy. Iran's military was loyal to the Shah personally — when he fell, they fell with him.

NATO membership was also crucial. Turkey joined in 1952, creating an institutional anchor to the West. Iran had no equivalent.

But starting around 2003, the balance began shifting.

Erdogan came to power — first as prime minister, then as president. Over 22 years, he has gradually reversed elements of the Ataturk revolution. He lifted the headscarf ban. Massively expanded religious schools. Added religious content to curricula. Converted Hagia Sophia back into a mosque. Weakened judicial independence. Pressured media. Imprisoned political opponents.

Is Erdogan slowly doing what Khomeini did suddenly? Analysts at RAND say not exactly. Erdogan is more populist than ideologue — "more an Ahmadinejad than a Khomeini." His inner circle cares more about power and money than revolutionary utopia. And ironically, despite 20-plus years of promoting religion, Turkey's younger generation is becoming more secular — just like Iran's youth under the ayatollahs.

The 2023 election was razor-close — Erdogan won 52 to 48. Real opposition exists. Real elections happen. But in March 2025, Erdogan arrested his strongest rival — Istanbul's mayor Imamoglu — along with hundreds of opposition members. Democracy is under serious strain.

Now, the question everyone asks: how does Turkey function with 30 to 85 percent inflation?

The lira went from 1.5 per dollar in 2010 to 44 per dollar in 2026 — a 96 percent depreciation. Inflation peaked at 85 percent in late 2022 and is still above 30 percent. The minimum wage barely covers basic food for a family.

But unlike Venezuela — where similar inflation destroyed the economy — Turkey keeps running. GDP grew 3.2 percent in 2024. A record 52.6 million tourists visited. Turkey became the world's fourth most-visited country, surpassing Italy. Manufacturing, automotive, textiles, defense — all operating. Turkey is the world's 11th largest arms exporter; Bayraktar drones changed modern warfare.

Why the difference? Venezuela had oil and nothing else. Turkey has a genuinely diversified economy. People adapt — they convert savings to dollars and gold. Turkish bank gold deposits surged 99 percent in one year. An estimated 7,000 to 8,000 tons of gold sits in Turkish homes — under pillows, literally.

Life is hard. Seventy-one percent of Turks say the country is in decline. Eighty-two percent rate the economy as "bad." Istanbul rents can exceed an entire monthly salary. But restaurants are open, construction continues, culture thrives. It's not Venezuelan despair — it's a grinding daily struggle with moments of normalcy and even joy.

And the identity question? Turkey applied to join the EU in 1987 — nearly 40 years ago. Still not a member. It's a NATO ally that buys Russian missiles. It hosts 2.3 million Syrian refugees. It plays Moscow, Washington, and Brussels against each other. Young Turks navigate between European secularism, Islamic heritage, and something uniquely Turkish that defies easy categorization.

What does Turkey tell us about the themes of this series?

It tells us secularization isn't irreversible — even Ataturk's radical reforms can be eroded over eight decades. But complete Islamization isn't inevitable either — even after 22 years of Erdogan, Turkey is not Iran. Elections still matter. Opposition still exists. Young people are secularizing despite their government, not because of it.

It tells us a country can survive severe inflation without collapsing — if the economy is diversified enough and the people are resilient enough. This isn't a satisfying answer. It's not "everything's fine" and it's not "everything's ruined." It's the messy reality most countries actually live in — not fairy tale, not nightmare, just daily struggle.

And it tells us that the question "are you Eastern or Western?" doesn't always have an answer. Maybe the most honest thing Turkey teaches is that some countries simply are both — and that's okay, even if it drives everyone crazy, including the Turks themselves.
