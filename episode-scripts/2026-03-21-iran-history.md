Let's start with the basics, because they matter more than you'd think.

Iran is big — about 636,000 square miles, roughly the size of Alaska. It sits on the Iranian Plateau, ringed by two major mountain ranges — the Zagros in the west and the Alborz along the Caspian coast, with vast deserts in the center. Population: around 90 to 92 million. Median age: 32 — meaning half the population was born after the revolution. About 77 percent live in cities. Tehran, the capital, has a metro area of over 16 million.

Here's something critically important that many people get wrong: Iranians are not Arabs. This is a common misconception, and Iranians are very sensitive about it. The dominant ethnic group is Persian, about 61 percent of the population. They speak Farsi — Persian — which is an Indo-European language. Linguistically, Farsi is more closely related to English and French than it is to Arabic. Yes, it uses the Arabic script, but it's a completely different language. Calling an Iranian an Arab is a bit like calling a Korean Chinese — same region, some cultural overlap, but entirely different people, language, and historical identity.

About 90 percent of Iranians are Shia Muslims, making Iran the world's largest Shia-majority country. Most Arab nations are Sunni-majority. The Sunni-Shia split dates back to the seventh century — a dispute over who should succeed the Prophet Muhammad — and it remains one of the deepest fault lines in Middle Eastern geopolitics today.

Now, here's what makes Iran's story so remarkable: the depth of its civilizational heritage.

Around 550 BC, Cyrus the Great established the Achaemenid Empire — stretching from Egypt to India, one of the largest empires the world had ever seen. The "Cyrus Cylinder," sometimes called the first human rights charter, proclaimed religious tolerance and freedom. Persian contributions to human knowledge are staggering. The mathematician al-Khwarizmi invented algebra — the English word comes from his book. The word "algorithm" comes from the Latin version of his name. In medicine, Ibn Sina — known in the West as Avicenna — wrote "The Canon of Medicine," which served as Europe's standard medical textbook for over 600 years.

Persian poetry is legendary. Rumi — a 13th-century Sufi mystic — is still one of the bestselling poets in the United States today. A verse by the poet Saadi is inscribed at the entrance to the United Nations: "Human beings are members of a whole." Even the English word "paradise" comes from the Persian "pairidaeza," meaning "walled garden."

This matters because it helps explain the psychology of modern Iran. This is a civilization that remembers being the center of the world. That memory shapes everything.

Now let's jump to the 1960s and 70s — the era that haunts Iran to this day.

Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi launched the "White Revolution" — a top-down modernization program. Land reform gave 2.5 million families their own farms. Women got the right to vote in 1963. The Family Protection Act of 1967 gave women legal rights in divorce and child custody. GDP growth hit 8 to 10 percent annually through the 1960s and 70s. By 1977, Iran was the world's 18th largest economy. A new middle class was forming — about 700,000 educated professionals.

If you've seen photos from 1970s Tehran, you'd think you were looking at any Western European city. Women in Western clothes, mixed-gender universities, nightclubs, cinemas. It looked like Iran had made an irreversible turn toward modernity.

But this coin had a very dark other side.

The Shah was an autocrat. His secret police, SAVAK — established with help from the CIA and Israel's Mossad in 1957 — conducted mass surveillance, torture, and extrajudicial killings. Political parties were dissolved. The press was censored. Nearly every social group — leftists, nationalists, intellectuals, students, religious leaders — was united by their hatred of SAVAK.

And there was a deep wound that never healed: in 1953, the CIA helped overthrow Iran's democratically elected prime minister, Mohammad Mosaddegh, and restored the Shah to power. Mosaddegh's crime? He had nationalized Iran's oil industry, threatening British petroleum interests. This event — confirmed by declassified CIA documents — shaped Iranian attitudes toward America for generations.

The oil wealth, meanwhile, was distributed grotesquely unevenly. Land reform gave farmers plots, but 73 percent got less than 15 acres — barely enough to survive. Many abandoned their insufficient plots and flooded into urban slums.

Into this volatile mix stepped Ayatollah Khomeini — a cleric exiled first to Iraq, then France. His genius was ambiguity. His public statements were vague enough that leftists, liberals, nationalists, and religious conservatives could all project their own hopes onto him.

In 1978, the pressure exploded. A cinema fire in Abadan killed 477 people. On September 8 — "Black Friday" — troops fired on protesters in Tehran. By January 1979, the Shah fled, never to return. On February 1, Khomeini flew into Tehran on an Air France 747. Millions lined the streets.

Then everything changed — fast.

By March 1979, women in government offices were required to wear hijab. The Family Protection Act was repealed. The legal marriage age for girls was lowered to nine. Women were banned from serving as judges. Universities were closed for three years for "cultural revolution." Religious minorities — especially Baha'is — faced systematic persecution.

For the urban middle class, their entire way of life was criminalized overnight. Beach vacations, mixed-gender socializing, going out without a headscarf — all of it suddenly illegal. Many who had supported the revolution felt profoundly betrayed. Khomeini had encouraged women to join the movement, letting them believe the revolution would bring more rights, not fewer.

Iranians developed what scholars call a "dual life" — strict compliance in public, a completely different existence behind closed doors. Private parties, satellite TV, underground music. The journalist Ramita Navai once wrote: "To survive in Tehran, you have to learn to lie."

Between 2 and 4 million Iranians chose a third path — they left. This became one of the largest brain drains in modern history.

About 750,000 Iranian-Americans live in the US today, nearly half in California. The Los Angeles area alone has an estimated 500,000 to 700,000 — the largest Iranian community outside Iran, earning L.A. the nickname "Tehrangeles." The community has produced extraordinary achievers — CEOs of major companies, Stanford professors, award-winning artists, prominent physicians. Iranian-Americans are among the most educated and highest-earning immigrant groups in the country.

What about Iran today — before the current military conflict? The picture was bleak.

The fertility rate crashed from six children per woman in the 1960s to about 1.6 today — below replacement level. Marriage rates dropped 40 percent in recent years. Every year, 150,000 to 180,000 science and technology professionals leave — an estimated economic loss of 50 to 70 billion dollars. Eighty percent of medical students are considering emigrating. A 2024 survey showed 89 percent of Iranians support democratic governance. Among 18-to-35-year-olds, 82 percent express "complete distrust" in national institutions.

Could Iran follow the China model — economic modernization while maintaining political control? Experts almost unanimously say no. China's Communist Party built its legitimacy on economic growth — a pragmatic social contract. Iran's regime claims divine authority. It doesn't need to prove itself through economic performance. That fundamental difference blocks Chinese-style reform.

And there's a deeper irony: the last Iranian leader who tried authoritarian modernization — the Shah himself — ended up creating the educated middle class that overthrew him. That historical lesson haunts any discussion of Iranian reform.

Iran before the 2026 conflict was already a country in slow-motion crisis: an aging theocratic leadership clinging to an ideology its people had abandoned; an economy hollowed by sanctions, corruption, and mismanagement; a demographic time bomb; and a young population that overwhelmingly wanted democracy, opportunity, and what they simply call "a normal life" — but most believed emigration was more realistic than reform.

That's Iran — a 2,500-year-old civilization trapped between its glorious past and its constrained present. A country where the word "paradise" was invented, but where millions dream of leaving.
