All three countries we've covered had deep ties to the Soviet Union. Cuba depended on Soviet subsidies for decades. Iran's revolution played out against the Cold War backdrop. Venezuela bought billions in Russian weapons. But none of them were directly controlled by the Soviets.

The countries the Soviet Union actually controlled were a cluster of Eastern European nations called "satellite states" — Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, East Germany, Romania, and today's subject: Bulgaria. They all broke free from communism around 1989. But thirty-five years later, their fates couldn't be more different.

Poland and the Czech Republic became economic powerhouses. Estonia invented Skype and built the world's most advanced digital government. Belarus is still a dictatorship. Ukraine tried to move West, and Russia invaded. Russia itself... well, you know.

Same starting point. Wildly different outcomes. Why?

Bulgaria's story might help answer that. Because it wasn't the star student — it stumbled badly in the 1990s, and for a long time, it looked like it might fail. But it didn't. It's a late bloomer that's finally coming into its own.

Let's start with the basics, because most people know almost nothing about Bulgaria.

Bulgaria sits in southeastern Europe, on the Balkan Peninsula. If you can't find it on a map, think: between Turkey and Romania, facing the Black Sea. It's about 43,000 square miles — roughly the size of Tennessee. Population: about 6.7 million. Capital: Sofia, about 1.7 million people.

The landscape is surprisingly diverse. Seventy percent hills, thirty percent mountains. The Balkan Mountains run east-to-west across the center — and fun fact, that mountain range is where the entire "Balkan Peninsula" gets its name. The word "Balkan" is Turkish for "mountain." In the southwest, Musala Peak rises to 9,596 feet — the highest point in the Balkans. The east has a Black Sea coastline with popular beach resorts. And tucked between two mountain ranges in the center, there's a place called the Rose Valley — more on that in a moment.

Bulgarians speak Bulgarian — a South Slavic language — and the dominant religion is Eastern Orthodox Christianity.

Now, here are some things about Bulgaria that almost nobody knows, but that Bulgarians are incredibly proud of.

First — the Cyrillic alphabet. You know, the script used for Russian, Ukrainian, Serbian, Mongolian — about 250 million people worldwide use it. Most people assume it's Russian. It's not. It was created in Bulgaria, in the ninth century, at the Preslav Literary School during the First Bulgarian Empire. When Bulgaria joined the EU in 2007, Cyrillic became the EU's third official script. May 24th is a national holiday in Bulgaria — Alphabet Day.

Second — yogurt. In 1905, a Bulgarian microbiologist named Stamen Grigorov discovered the specific bacterium that turns milk into yogurt. It was named Lactobacillus bulgaricus in his honor. Here's where it gets fun: at the 1970 Osaka World Expo, representatives from Japan's Meiji dairy company visited the Bulgarian pavilion, tasted authentic yogurt, and were blown away. In 1973, Meiji launched "Meiji Bulgaria Yogurt" — it's still one of Japan's bestselling yogurt brands today. Japan even has an annual "Bulgarian Yogurt Day."

Third — rose oil. That Rose Valley I mentioned? For over 400 years, it's been the world center for rose oil production. It takes 3,000 to 4,000 kilograms of rose petals to produce one kilogram of rose oil — which sells for up to 15,000 dollars per kilo. Chanel, Dior, Louis Vuitton, and Gucci all source from Bulgaria.

Fourth — lavender. Bulgaria has quietly overtaken France as the world's largest producer of lavender oil.

Fifth — and this one blows my mind — a Bulgarian folk song is literally traveling beyond our solar system right now. In 1977, NASA included a song called "Izlel ye Delyo Haydutin," sung by Valya Balkanska, on the Voyager Golden Record. If aliens ever hear the human voice, one of the voices they'll hear will be Bulgarian.

And some notable Bulgarians you might not know about: John Atanasoff co-invented the first electronic digital computer. Christo was the artist who wrapped the Reichstag in fabric. Stefka Kostadinova has held the women's high jump world record since 1987 — nearly 40 years and counting.

Oh, and one cultural quirk: in Bulgaria, shaking your head means "yes" and nodding means "no." The opposite of everywhere else. One popular — though unverified — legend says this dates to Ottoman rule: Bulgarians swapped the gestures so they could nod "yes" to converting to Islam while actually meaning "no."

Now let's get into the story.

Bulgaria is one of Europe's oldest states. Founded in 681 AD — before France, England, or Germany existed as countries. The First Bulgarian Empire, under Tsar Simeon the Great, stretched from the Adriatic to the Black Sea and rivaled the Byzantine Empire. The Cyrillic alphabet was born during this golden age.

Then came the Ottomans. From roughly 1396 to 1878 — nearly 500 years of Ottoman rule. Half a millennium. Through all of it, the Bulgarian Orthodox Church preserved the language, culture, and national identity.

In 1878, Russia defeated the Ottomans and liberated Bulgaria. March 3rd — Liberation Day — is Bulgaria's national holiday. This is why Bulgaria's relationship with Russia is so complicated: Russia is the liberator. But Russia also became the controller.

During World War II, Bulgaria allied with the Axis powers. But there's a remarkable chapter: Bulgaria refused to deport its own 48,000 Jewish citizens to Nazi death camps. Through the efforts of a parliamentarian named Dimitar Peshev, the Orthodox Church, and public pressure, they were saved. Bulgaria did, tragically, hand over more than 11,000 Jews from territories it occupied in Greece and Yugoslavia — but it protected its own.

In 1944, a Soviet-backed coup installed a communist government. For the next 45 years, Bulgaria was the most loyal Soviet satellite — nicknamed "the 16th Soviet Republic." Its leader, Todor Zhivkov, ruled from 1954 to 1989 — the longest-serving leader in the Eastern Bloc. Declassified documents reveal he even proposed that Bulgaria formally join the Soviet Union.

In November 1989, the same month the Berlin Wall fell, Communist Party members voted to remove Zhivkov. Unlike Poland or Czechoslovakia, where people power drove the change, in Bulgaria it was an inside job — the party replaced its own leader.

Then came the 1990s — the darkest chapter of modern Bulgarian history.

The transition to a market economy went badly wrong. Privatization enriched insiders while ordinary people got nothing. Organized crime — often run by former security services personnel — took over large parts of the economy. In 1996, nine banks collapsed. The currency went from 70 leva per dollar to over 3,000. Monthly inflation hit 242 percent in February 1997. Food and fuel shortages. Unpaid wages. People queuing for bread in the freezing cold.

This is when the brain drain began in earnest. Young, educated Bulgarians left for Western Europe and North America. About 1.7 million Bulgarians — 20 percent of the nation — live abroad today.

But here's the turning point. In 1997, a new government introduced a currency board, pegging the lev to the Deutsche Mark — and later the euro. The economy stabilized. And Bulgaria made a clear strategic choice: West, not East.

NATO membership in 2004. EU membership in 2007. Schengen zone in January 2025 — after a 13-year wait. And then the biggest milestone: euro adoption on January 1, 2026, making Bulgaria the 21st eurozone member. Foreign Policy magazine described it as "a setback for Russia" — because it anchors Bulgaria deeper into the Western economic system.

What does Bulgaria look like today?

GDP growth at 3 percent — above the EU average. Wages rising over 10 percent annually. The tech sector's revenue tripled in seven years, reaching 2.5 billion euros — Sofia is called "the Silicon Valley of Southeastern Europe," with 10,000 ICT companies. Real estate prices are climbing but still remarkably affordable by European standards — about 2,000 euros per square meter in Sofia, versus over 5,000 in Paris. Tourism is booming — 13.6 million visitors in 2025. A digital nomad visa launched in 2025. And some of those Bulgarians who left are starting to come back — bringing Western experience and capital with them.

But Bulgaria's problems are real too. The population has dropped from nearly 9 million in 1987 to 6.7 million — a 28 percent decline, one of the steepest in the world. Every day, about 163 babies are born and 280 people die. Projections suggest just 5.4 million by 2050. Politically, Bulgaria has been chaotic — eight parliamentary elections in five years. Corruption remains a serious issue. And the gap between Sofia and the rest of the country is enormous — Sofia generates 41 percent of national GDP, while the northwestern region has the lowest per capita GDP in the entire EU.

The Russia relationship continues to evolve. Russia liberated Bulgaria in 1878. Russia controlled it for 45 years under communism. But since the 2022 Ukraine invasion, diplomatic relations have hit historic lows. Yet public opinion remains divided — a 2026 survey showed 52.5 percent of Bulgarians would support negotiations on Russia's terms to end the Ukraine conflict. That tension — gratitude and distance, historical memory and present reality — is central to understanding Bulgaria.

So what does Bulgaria's story tell us about the bigger picture?

Among all the countries that escaped communism around 1989, the ones that chose to integrate with the EU and NATO — Poland, the Czech Republic, the Baltics, Bulgaria — almost without exception achieved economic growth and institutional improvement. The ones that tried to straddle East and West — like Serbia — or leaned back toward Russia — like Belarus — either stagnated or regressed.

Three factors made the difference. Direction: choosing West over East. Institutional anchoring: EU membership isn't just money, it's a set of rules — independent courts, minority rights, anti-corruption standards. And time: Bulgaria took 35 years to get from 1989 to where it is today. Transformation isn't overnight. It takes at least a generation.
