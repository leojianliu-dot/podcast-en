Cuba is one of those countries everyone thinks they know — cigars, classic cars, communism, the Castros. But the real story is far more fascinating, and far more tragic, than the cliches suggest. Because Cuba wasn't always poor. In the 1950s, it was one of the wealthiest countries in Latin America. And it has a "twin sister" — Puerto Rico — that makes for one of the most striking natural experiments in modern history.

Let's start with the basics.

Cuba is the largest island in the Caribbean — about 43,000 square miles, roughly the size of Virginia. It's shaped like a long crocodile, stretching 780 miles east to west but very narrow — just 120 miles at its widest, 19 miles at its thinnest. Population: about 10 million, though that number is dropping fast. The capital, Havana, sits on the northwest coast.

Here's a fact that changes everything about Cuba's story: it's incredibly close to the United States. From Key West, Florida to Cuba — just 93 miles. Key West to Havana — 106 miles. That's roughly the distance from San Francisco to Sacramento. On a clear day, you can almost see Cuba from Florida. This proximity has defined the entire relationship — the drama, the paranoia, the missiles.

Now, before we get into Cuba's own story, let me introduce its twin.

Cuba and Puerto Rico are eerily similar. Both are Caribbean islands. Both were Spanish colonies for nearly 400 years — Cuba from 1492, Puerto Rico from 1493. Both populations are a mix of Spanish, African, and indigenous Taino heritage. Both built their economies on sugar cane and slave labor. Both speak Spanish. Both are Catholic.

Then, in 1898, fate intervened. The Spanish-American War ended with Spain losing both islands to the United States in the same treaty — the Treaty of Paris. Same war, same treaty, two islands — and from that moment, their paths diverged completely.

Puerto Rico became a US territory. Puerto Ricans got US citizenship in 1917. They use the dollar, receive federal funds — Medicaid, Social Security, food stamps. They can freely move to the mainland. By 2024, Puerto Rico's GDP per capita was about 31,000 dollars.

Cuba went independent — nominally. The US imposed the Platt Amendment, giving itself the right to intervene militarily in Cuba at any time and requiring Cuba to lease land for a naval base. That base — Guantanamo Bay — still exists today. Annual rent: 4,085 dollars. Cuba hasn't cashed a US rent check since the revolution.

So what was pre-revolution Cuba actually like? This might surprise you.

In 1958, Cuba's per capita GDP ranked third in Latin America, behind only Venezuela and Uruguay. Adjusted for purchasing power, Cuba's income exceeded Ireland's and Austria's, was nearly double Spain's, and surpassed Japan's. Literacy rate: 76 percent, fourth in Latin America. Life expectancy: 63 years, third in the Western Hemisphere. Infant mortality: 13th lowest in the world — lower than France or Germany. Television ownership per capita was fifth globally, first in Latin America.

Havana was called the "Paris of the Caribbean." Gorgeous colonial architecture, upscale restaurants, casinos, jazz clubs. American tourists flocked there. The American Mafia built a massive casino empire — mob boss Meyer Lansky was appointed the official gambling advisor by dictator Batista. By the late 1950s, the Mafia had invested about 950 million dollars in Havana.

But — just like the Shah's Iran — these impressive numbers masked brutal inequality.

Who was enjoying the prosperity? Mainly the urban white upper-middle class, American companies, and the mob. Outside the cities? A different world. Overall literacy was 76 percent, but rural illiteracy hit 42 percent. Sugar cane cutters worked only four months a year, chronically malnourished and indebted. Eighty-five percent of farmers worked land they didn't own.

American companies controlled 90 percent of Cuba's mines, 80 percent of its utilities, 50 percent of its railroads, 40 percent of its sugar industry, and 25 percent of its bank deposits. The country's economic lifeline was in foreign hands.

This is the combustible mix — enough prosperity to create awareness, enough inequality to create desperation — that makes revolutions possible.

Fidel Castro came from a wealthy family in eastern Cuba. He studied law at the University of Havana, where he absorbed leftist and anti-imperialist ideas. After a failed 1953 attack on the Moncada Barracks and two years in prison, he fled to Mexico, where he met Argentine revolutionary Che Guevara. In November 1956, Castro returned to Cuba with about 80 fighters. Batista's army nearly wiped them out immediately — only about 20 survived, including Castro and Guevara. They retreated into the Sierra Maestra mountains and waged two years of guerrilla warfare.

On January 1, 1959, Batista fled with over 300 million dollars in personal wealth. The revolution had won.

Was Castro always a communist? That's one of the most debated questions in Cold War history. The historical consensus is that regardless of Castro's private beliefs, American hostility was the primary catalyst pushing Cuba into the Soviet orbit. The failed CIA-backed Bay of Pigs invasion in April 1961 sealed the deal. By December 1961, Castro declared himself a Marxist-Leninist.

Then came the Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962 — the closest humanity has ever come to nuclear war. Soviet nuclear missiles, 90 miles from Florida. President Kennedy chose a naval blockade over airstrikes. Soviet ships headed toward the blockade line. The world held its breath. Eventually Khrushchev agreed to remove the missiles; the US promised not to invade Cuba and secretly agreed to remove its own missiles from Turkey.

Post-revolution Cuba was transformed. American companies were nationalized. Land reform seized all farms over 33 acres. The US imposed a full trade embargo — which began in 1962 and continues today, over 64 years later, the longest trade embargo in modern history. The UN General Assembly condemns it nearly every year by votes around 187 to 2.

But the revolution also produced remarkable achievements in education and healthcare. In 1961, nearly 200,000 young volunteers fanned across the country and taught about 700,000 people to read. Literacy jumped from 60 to 96 percent in one year. UNESCO called it a model literacy campaign. Cuba today has one of the highest doctor-to-patient ratios in the world and sends medical teams to dozens of countries.

The human cost, however, has been staggering. Political repression, single-party rule, no free press, no independent judiciary. Wages average 20 to 30 dollars a month. The basic food basket costs far more than anyone earns. Eighty-nine percent of Cubans live in extreme poverty by some measures. The infrastructure is literally crumbling — frequent blackouts, water shortages, collapsing buildings.

The Cuban diaspora is massive. About 2.5 million people of Cuban descent live in the US, concentrated in South Florida. Miami's Little Havana is a cultural powerhouse. Cuban-Americans have produced US senators, Fortune 500 executives, Grammy winners, and supreme court justices. The community wields enormous political influence — no presidential candidate can ignore the Cuban-American vote in Florida.

Cuba today stands at a crossroads. In March 2026, leader Diaz-Canel made a rare public call for "immediate reform of the economic and social model." But hardliners are pushing back. The question is no longer whether Cuba will change, but what will replace the current system and how turbulent that transition will be.

The Puerto Rico comparison is the final, devastating data point. Two islands, nearly identical starting conditions, separated by one treaty in 1898. Puerto Rico's per capita GDP is now more than three times Cuba's. Both islands are losing their people — Cuba to poverty and repression, Puerto Rico to economic stagnation and colonial marginalization. Neither path led to an ideal outcome. But if you asked Cubans and Puerto Ricans whether they'd trade places — with 89 percent of Cubans in extreme poverty — the answer isn't hard to guess.
